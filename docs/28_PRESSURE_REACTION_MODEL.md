# Pressure Reaction Model

## Purpose

This document describes how the city, organizations, and law enforcement
react when pressure accumulates in the world.

Pressure is the force that pushes back against criminal activity.
Without pressure, the player faces no meaningful resistance.
With pressure, every decision carries real consequences.

## Core idea

Pressure is not a single number.
It is a layered state of tension distributed across the city.

Different actors feel pressure differently and react in different ways.
The city does not simply punish the player — it responds as a living system.

## Pressure sources

Pressure builds from:
- criminal operations (especially violent or visible ones)
- heat accumulated in districts
- rival organization activity
- economic damage to the city
- public fear levels
- political exposure
- media attention (implied by event chains)
- informants and leaks inside organizations

## Pressure types

### Law enforcement pressure
Direct police and institutional response.
- increased patrols in a district
- targeted surveillance on a known operation
- arrest sweep of low-level crew
- investigation opened on a lieutenant or boss
- asset seizure
- coordinated raid
- undercover infiltration

### Civilian pressure
The population of a district reacting to instability.
- reduced cooperation with criminal activity
- increased reporting to police
- business relocation or closure
- public protests or media coverage
- drop in recruitment pool availability
- vigilante or self-defense activity in extreme cases

### Political pressure
Institutional actors responding to public or economic damage.
- political mandate to clean up a district
- new laws or crackdown policies
- increased law enforcement budget for an area
- pressure on corrupt officials to sever ties
- high-profile prosecution push

### Rival pressure
Other organizations responding to the player's growth.
- contested operations become more dangerous
- rivals form temporary alliances against a dominant player
- rivals leak information to police
- rivals attempt to destabilize player's territory
- rivals move into districts weakened by heat

## Pressure thresholds

Pressure should have thresholds that trigger escalating reactions.

### District level

| Threshold | State | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Low | Calm | Normal operation success rates |
| Moderate | Tense | Police patrols increase, civilian caution rises |
| High | Hot | Operations harder, crew exposure risk up |
| Critical | Crackdown | Active raids, arrests, district partially locked |
| Breaking point | Collapse | District destabilized, all organizations suffer |

### Organization level

| Threshold | State | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Low heat | Shadow | Operating freely, low visibility |
| Medium heat | Noticed | Some operations monitored |
| High heat | Targeted | Leadership under surveillance |
| Critical heat | Hunted | Active investigation, assets at risk |

## Pressure decay

Pressure should not stay permanently at peak.
It decays over time when the organization goes quiet.

Decay principles:
- no operations = heat drops each tick
- low-visibility operations slow decay but do not reverse it
- bribery and corruption payments accelerate decay
- critical failures or violent events reset decay progress
- a district recovers faster if criminal influence is removed

## Reaction chaining

Pressure reactions can trigger further reactions.

Examples:
- police raid → crew arrested → organization weakened → rival moves in
- civilian fear spike → business closure → district income drops → organization loses revenue
- political pressure → corrupt official cuts contact → protection lost → exposure increases
- rival tip-off → investigation opened → operation disrupted → heat spikes further

Chains should feel emergent, not scripted.
The same initial pressure event can cascade differently depending on world state.

## Player response options

The player should have tools to manage pressure before it reaches critical levels.

### Defensive responses
- lay low — halt aggressive operations, let heat decay
- bribe officials — reduce police activity in a district
- destroy evidence — close off an investigation before it escalates
- extract exposed crew — prevent arrests before they happen
- relocate operations — shift activity to a less-watched district

### Active responses
- misdirection — frame a rival to redirect police attention
- informant removal — eliminate the source of leaks
- political leverage — use influence to suppress a political push
- public normalization — use front businesses to project legitimacy
- media narrative — create events that shift public perception

## AI organization reactions to pressure

Other organizations also experience pressure and react to it.

- an organization under heavy heat may go quiet or seek a truce
- a weakened organization may offer the player an alliance
- a rival under no pressure may become aggressive and expansionist
- organizations can collapse entirely if pressure becomes unmanageable
- smaller organizations may be absorbed by stronger ones during crackdowns

## Pressure and district interaction

Pressure does not exist in isolation from districts.

- a high-crime district builds pressure faster
- a highly corrupt district absorbs more pressure before reacting
- a wealthy district triggers stronger political reactions at lower thresholds
- an industrial district reacts more through logistics disruption than police
- a poor district may absorb violence longer before civilian reaction kicks in

## Pressure as a game clock

Pressure functions as a soft time limit on player aggression.

The player cannot endlessly expand without consequences.
Every successful run of operations pushes the world closer to a reaction.
The game rewards players who know when to push and when to pull back.

This creates a rhythm:
- operate → accumulate heat → manage pressure → stabilize → operate again

A player who ignores pressure will eventually face a crisis they cannot recover from.
A player who manages pressure well will build durable, long-term power.

## Design principle

Pressure is well designed if it makes the player feel the weight of their decisions.
It should not feel like a punishment timer.
It should feel like a city waking up to what is happening inside it.
