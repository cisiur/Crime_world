# Pressure Reaction Model

## Purpose

This document describes how the city, organizations, institutions, and important characters react when criminal activity becomes visible or disruptive.

Pressure is the systemic pushback created by the player's actions and by the actions of other organizations.

## Core terminology

To avoid ambiguity, the project uses the following terms:

- **Pressure** — the total systemic pushback generated by crime, instability, public fear, politics, rivals, and institutions.
- **Exposure** — how visible a specific person, operation, business, or organization is.
- **Investigation** — a concrete law-enforcement process aimed at a target.
- **Tension** — the current instability of a district.
- **Personal exposure** — how strongly a specific character is connected to criminal activity.

The term `heat` should not be used as a universal label for all of these systems.

## Core idea

Pressure is not one global number.
It is a layered state distributed across:
- districts,
- organizations,
- operations,
- businesses,
- and characters.

Different actors feel pressure differently and react in different ways.
The city does not simply punish the player. It responds as a living system.

## Pressure sources

Pressure builds from:
- visible or violent criminal operations,
- repeated activity in the same area,
- rival organization activity,
- economic damage,
- public fear,
- political exposure,
- media attention,
- informants and leaks,
- active investigations,
- internal instability.

## Pressure types

### Law-enforcement pressure

Direct institutional response:
- increased patrols,
- targeted surveillance,
- arrest sweeps,
- formal investigations,
- asset seizure,
- coordinated raids,
- undercover infiltration.

### Civilian pressure

Population and business response:
- reduced cooperation,
- increased reporting,
- business closure or relocation,
- public protests,
- lower recruitment availability,
- private security or self-defense activity.

### Political pressure

Institutional and political response:
- public cleanup mandates,
- budget increases,
- pressure on corrupt officials,
- high-profile prosecutions,
- new enforcement priorities.

### Rival pressure

Other organizations reacting to growth:
- retaliation,
- temporary coalitions,
- information leaks,
- territorial challenges,
- entry into weakened districts.

### Internal pressure

Pressure inside an organization:
- declining loyalty,
- fear of arrest,
- leadership disputes,
- panic after losses,
- faction formation,
- betrayal risk.

## Pressure states

### District pressure states

| State | Meaning | Typical effects |
|---|---|---|
| Calm | Low tension and low visibility | Normal operations |
| Tense | Growing concern | More patrols, cautious civilians |
| Hot | Active instability | Higher risk and lower cooperation |
| Crackdown | Coordinated response | Raids, arrests, restrictions |
| Destabilized | Local systems breaking down | All organizations suffer |

### Organization exposure states

| State | Meaning | Typical effects |
|---|---|---|
| Hidden | Low institutional awareness | Broad freedom of action |
| Noticed | Pattern detected | Soft monitoring |
| Targeted | Organization identified | Investigations and surveillance |
| Hunted | Leadership and assets prioritized | Raids, seizures, arrests |

### Personal exposure states

| State | Meaning | Typical effects |
|---|---|---|
| Unknown | No meaningful connection | Minimal personal risk |
| Suspected | Weak connection | Occasional surveillance |
| Identified | Known criminal link | Monitored contacts and movement |
| Wanted | Active pursuit | Arrest and travel risk |

## Persistence and decay

Pressure does not disappear as one block.
Different layers decay differently.

### Temporary pressure can decay

Examples:
- patrol intensity,
- public panic,
- short-term rival alertness,
- operation visibility.

### Persistent consequences remain

Examples:
- open investigations,
- collected evidence,
- criminal records,
- surviving witnesses,
- political grudges,
- rival hostility,
- damaged relationships.

Going quiet should reduce immediate exposure, but it should not erase history.

## Investigation model

An investigation is a concrete process with:
- target,
- evidence level,
- institutional priority,
- assigned resources,
- current stage,
- possible outcomes.

Possible stages:
1. suspicion,
2. evidence gathering,
3. surveillance,
4. warrant or intervention preparation,
5. raid, arrest, or seizure.

Investigations can be slowed, redirected, exposed, or collapsed, but should not vanish automatically because the player waits.

## Reaction chaining

Pressure reactions can trigger further reactions.

Examples:
- raid -> arrests -> organization weakness -> rival expansion,
- public fear -> business closures -> district decline -> recruitment growth,
- political pressure -> corrupt contact withdraws -> investigation accelerates,
- rival leak -> investigation opens -> operation fails -> exposure rises.

The same initial event should cascade differently depending on the world state.

## Player response options

### Defensive responses

- reduce visible activity,
- relocate exposed operations,
- extract compromised members,
- protect witnesses or contacts,
- strengthen legal cover,
- isolate vulnerable assets.

### Active responses

- destroy or discredit evidence,
- expose rival activity,
- redirect investigators,
- use political leverage,
- replace compromised personnel,
- improve public legitimacy,
- negotiate temporary stability.

## AI reactions

Organizations under pressure may:
- reduce activity,
- move operations,
- seek truces,
- sacrifice exposed members,
- increase corruption spending,
- retaliate against suspected informants,
- split or collapse.

## Pressure and districts

District context changes how pressure behaves:
- wealthy districts trigger stronger political reactions,
- corrupt districts absorb more short-term pressure,
- industrial districts suffer through logistics disruption,
- poor districts may tolerate violence longer but destabilize faster,
- high-surveillance districts convert exposure into investigations more efficiently.

## Pressure as pacing

Pressure creates a rhythm:

operate -> gain value -> increase exposure -> face reactions -> adapt -> stabilize -> operate again

The goal is not to punish activity.
The goal is to force the player to vary methods, build resilience, and decide when to push or retreat.

## Design principle

Pressure is well designed when it creates understandable consequences, meaningful counterplay, and lasting history.

It should feel like the city waking up to what is happening inside it, not like an arbitrary punishment timer.

## Open Questions
