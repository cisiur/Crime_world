# Living World Behavior

## Purpose

This document describes what the world does when the player is not directly issuing commands.

The world must feel alive, persistent, and capable of generating change on its own.

## Core idea

The world should not wait for the player.
It should continue to move through its own incentives, problems, and conflicts.

## Main world actors

The living world is driven by:
- residents
- workers
- businesses
- criminal organizations
- police
- politicians
- informants
- key NPCs
- rival groups
- district-level conditions

## Daily behavior cycle

At a high level, the world should repeatedly do the following:
1. evaluate local conditions
2. choose priorities
3. perform actions
4. update consequences
5. create new opportunities and risks

## Resident behavior

Residents influence:
- demand
- fear
- recruitment potential
- neighborhood stability
- business activity
- crime tolerance

Residents do not need deep individual simulation in the MVP, but district-level resident behavior should still exist as a source of pressure and opportunity.

## Business behavior

Businesses should respond to:
- demand
- security
- corruption
- logistics quality
- criminal pressure
- neighborhood stability
- public fear

Businesses may grow, shrink, relocate, close, or become front businesses depending on conditions.

## Organization behavior

Organizations should behave according to their priorities.
They may:
- expand territory
- defend assets
- recruit members
- build income streams
- retaliate
- negotiate
- infiltrate rivals
- form alliances
- absorb weaker groups

## Police behavior

Police should not respond to everything equally.
They should prioritize:
- visible crimes
- high-value cases
- politically important districts
- repeated offenders
- major organizations
- public scandals

Police behavior is influenced by:
- budgets
- corruption
- pressure from the public
- political support
- internal quality
- active investigations

## District behavior

Districts should drift over time depending on local conditions.
A district can become:
- safer
- more dangerous
- more corrupt
- more profitable
- more monitored
- more unstable
- more attractive to criminals

## Background world motion

Even if the player takes no action, the following should still happen:
- some businesses change state
- rivals reposition
- police investigations advance
- reputation shifts slightly
- pressure rises or falls in certain areas
- opportunities appear or disappear

## Event generation

The world should periodically generate events based on current state.

Examples:
- a district becomes unstable
- a business owner is vulnerable
- a rival group expands
- a police raid happens elsewhere
- a local official changes stance
- a supply route becomes more expensive

## Story value

Living world behavior is important because it creates the raw material for emergent stories.
The player should feel like they entered a moving ecosystem, not a fixed board.

## Design principle

The world should always be doing something that matters, even when the player is not looking directly at it.
