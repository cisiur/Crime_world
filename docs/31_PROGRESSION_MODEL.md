# Progression Model

## Purpose

This document defines how the player and their organization grow from a single unknown character into a major criminal power.

Progression in CrimeWorld should not be a conventional level grind.
It should emerge from increased capability, access, influence, resilience, and strategic reach.

## Core principle

The player does not become stronger because a level number increases.
The player becomes stronger because the world treats them differently and because their organization can do things it could not do before.

Progression should therefore be visible through:
- stronger people,
- better assets,
- wider contacts,
- safer operations,
- broader influence,
- deeper intelligence,
- stronger legal cover,
- and greater organizational capacity.

## Progression layers

CrimeWorld uses multiple connected progression layers.

### 1. Personal progression

The player character grows through:
- reputation,
- personal network,
- knowledge,
- credibility,
- fear or respect,
- legal cover,
- and access to important people.

Personal progression affects:
- negotiation options,
- recruitment quality,
- diplomatic credibility,
- access to rare opportunities,
- and personal survival.

### 2. Organizational progression

The organization grows through:
- more trusted members,
- a deeper hierarchy,
- more parallel operation capacity,
- better logistics,
- stronger internal security,
- more stable income,
- and better crisis recovery.

Organizational progression affects what the player can sustain, not only what they can attempt.

### 3. Economic progression

The player advances economically through:
- recurring income,
- diversified revenue,
- legal front businesses,
- strategic assets,
- supply chain control,
- and reduced dependency on one fragile source of money.

Economic progression should reduce vulnerability while increasing exposure and management complexity.

### 4. Territorial progression

Territorial progression moves through:
- a single safe location,
- local presence,
- district influence,
- contested district control,
- city-wide reach,
- regional influence,
- and eventually multi-city networks.

Control should not be purely binary.
The player may have weak, partial, shared, or dominant influence in an area.

### 5. Institutional progression

The organization can gradually gain access to:
- corrupt officials,
- legal professionals,
- police informants,
- political intermediaries,
- media contacts,
- and financial protection.

This layer increases the organization's ability to survive pressure rather than simply generate more profit.

## Progression gates

New capabilities should be gated by real conditions in the simulation.

Possible gates include:
- personal reputation,
- organizational reputation,
- trusted leadership capacity,
- stable income,
- district influence,
- required contacts,
- access to specific business sectors,
- intelligence quality,
- legal cover,
- and acceptable pressure levels.

A capability should unlock because the organization has become able to support it, not because an arbitrary level was reached.

## Capability tiers

The following tiers describe the broad shape of progression.
They are design states, not mandatory visible player levels.

### Tier 0: Unknown individual

The player has:
- no organization,
- minimal money,
- one or two weak contacts,
- no territory,
- no meaningful protection.

Available actions are personal, small, and dangerous.

### Tier 1: Small crew

The player has:
- a few trusted people,
- one hideout,
- basic recurring income,
- limited local reputation,
- and the ability to run one or two simple operations.

The main challenge is survival and trust.

### Tier 2: Local organization

The organization has:
- a simple hierarchy,
- multiple crews,
- several income sources,
- local business influence,
- and recognizable presence in one or more districts.

The main challenge is managing pressure and rivals.

### Tier 3: City power

The organization has:
- district-level control,
- strategic businesses,
- institutional contacts,
- better intelligence,
- and enough leadership to run parallel plans.

The main challenge is overextension, diplomacy, and internal stability.

### Tier 4: Regional network

The organization has:
- influence across several cities or a wider region,
- inter-city logistics,
- subordinate groups or strong allies,
- and meaningful political or institutional leverage.

The main challenge is coordination and maintaining control at distance.

### Tier 5: Major syndicate

The organization has:
- international reach,
- resilient legal and illegal networks,
- major political influence,
- and the ability to affect entire sectors or regions.

The main challenge is survival against coalitions, investigations, succession crises, and structural fragmentation.

## Unlock philosophy

Unlocks should mostly provide new decisions rather than direct numerical superiority.

Good unlocks include:
- access to a new operation type,
- the ability to delegate a district,
- use of a new logistics route,
- recruitment of a specialist role,
- a new diplomatic agreement type,
- a stronger form of legal cover,
- or a new way to manage pressure.

Weak unlocks include:
- flat percentage bonuses with no new interaction,
- arbitrary level requirements,
- and passive rewards that do not change strategy.

## Progression through people

People are one of the most important progression resources.

A more powerful organization needs:
- trusted lieutenants,
- competent operators,
- specialists,
- intermediaries,
- and possible successors.

This means that losing one important person can temporarily reverse progression even when money and territory remain intact.

## Progression through information

Knowledge is a permanent strategic advantage unless the world changes.

The player can progress by learning:
- rival weaknesses,
- police priorities,
- hidden relationships,
- district opportunities,
- supply routes,
- and vulnerable institutions.

Information progression should open safer or more precise options.

## Progression through reputation

Reputation should create both benefits and costs.

A feared organization may gain:
- easier intimidation,
- faster submission,
- stronger recruitment appeal,
- and more diplomatic leverage.

But it may also suffer:
- stronger coalition pressure,
- higher visibility,
- worse civilian cooperation,
- and greater law enforcement priority.

A discreet organization may gain fewer immediate benefits but remain harder to target.

## Progression and pressure

Growth should increase both capability and resistance from the world.

As the player grows:
- operations become larger,
- rewards increase,
- more actors notice the organization,
- internal coordination becomes harder,
- and mistakes have wider consequences.

Progression must not remove tension.
It should change the type of tension.

## Setbacks and recovery

Progression should not be strictly linear.

The player may lose:
- territory,
- people,
- businesses,
- relationships,
- reputation,
- or legal protection.

A setback should reduce capability without automatically ending the campaign.
Recovery should be possible through:
- retreat,
- restructuring,
- diplomacy,
- replacement leadership,
- lower-profile operations,
- and rebuilding local influence.

## Sandbox goals

CrimeWorld is an open-ended sandbox.
The progression system should support multiple player-defined ambitions, such as:
- dominate one city,
- monopolize a sector,
- build the richest organization,
- become politically untouchable,
- create a durable dynasty,
- become feared globally,
- or remain a hidden network with minimal exposure.

The game should suggest goals without forcing a single progression path.

## Player feedback

The player should understand why a capability is available or unavailable.

The UI should communicate:
- current strengths,
- missing prerequisites,
- organizational bottlenecks,
- new opportunities,
- and risks created by growth.

The system should avoid unexplained locks.

## Design principle

Progression is well designed if the player gains new strategic options while also inheriting new responsibilities and risks.

If growth only makes existing actions easier, progression is too shallow.
If growth adds complexity without meaningful power, progression becomes frustrating.

## Open Questions

- Which progression layers should be visible as explicit meters and which should remain emergent?
- Should capability tiers be visible to the player or exist only as internal design categories?
- How much progression can be inherited by a successor?
- Which capabilities should require a specific specialist NPC rather than a general organization threshold?
- How quickly should the game transition from personal actions to delegated organization management?

## Future Extensions

- Multi-city progression and regional administration.
- Formal subordinate organizations and tribute networks.
- Dynasty legacy traits carried between successors.
- Historical-era progression if campaigns span many decades.
- Organization doctrines or cultural traditions that shape available progression paths.
