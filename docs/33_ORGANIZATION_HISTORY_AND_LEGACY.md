# Organization History and Legacy

## Purpose

This document defines how CrimeWorld records the long-term history of an organization and turns campaign events into a persistent chronicle.

The system exists to make endless sandbox play meaningful across decades, leadership changes, collapses, recoveries, rivalries, and optional milestones.

## Core principle

History is not a separate progression tree.

The organization does not gain arbitrary permanent bonuses merely because an event was recorded.
History preserves context, identity, and consequences.

Legacy should make the player feel that the organization has existed in the world, changed it, survived it, and been changed by it.

## What history records

The campaign should maintain structured records of important events.

These may include:
- organization founding,
- changes of name, symbol, doctrine, or headquarters,
- each boss's reign,
- succession and regency periods,
- major internal power struggles,
- district expansion and territorial collapse,
- important businesses and strategic assets,
- major operations,
- wars, truces, alliances, and betrayals,
- investigations, raids, arrests, and prosecutions,
- legendary members and notorious traitors,
- optional milestone completions,
- near-destruction and recovery,
- and voluntary campaign retirement.

The history system should not record every routine tick or minor transaction.
It should select events that meaningfully changed the organization or the city.

## Historical event model

Each historical entry should contain enough structure to be useful beyond a text log.

Suggested properties:
- date or simulation time,
- event category,
- headline,
- short description,
- involved characters,
- involved organizations,
- affected districts, businesses, or routes,
- immediate outcome,
- long-term significance,
- visibility,
- confidence or information quality,
- and links to related events.

Structured entries allow the same history to support:
- a chronological timeline,
- character biographies,
- organization statistics,
- district histories,
- rival relationship summaries,
- and end-of-campaign reports.

## Significance

Events should receive a significance score or category.

Possible levels:
- Minor — locally notable but unlikely to define a reign.
- Important — changed an operation, relationship, district, or key asset.
- Major — altered organizational direction or city balance.
- Historic — defined an era, succession, collapse, recovery, or major milestone.

Significance should be derived from actual impact, not only from event type.

For example:
- a small robbery is normally minor,
- the same robbery may become major if its failure exposes the future boss,
- a single betrayal may be historic if it causes a city-wide war,
- losing a district may be important or historic depending on its role in the organization.

## Reigns and eras

The organization history should be divided into leadership reigns and broader eras.

### Boss reign

Each boss should have a record containing:
- beginning and end of reign,
- method of accession,
- starting and ending organization state,
- major achievements,
- major failures,
- important allies and rivals,
- personal reputation,
- leadership style as demonstrated through play,
- succession preparation,
- and cause of removal, retirement, imprisonment, or death.

A reign ends when leadership permanently transfers.
Temporary imprisonment, injury, or regency does not necessarily end it.

### Organization era

An era describes a broader strategic period that may span one or more bosses.

Examples:
- founding years,
- district expansion,
- commercial consolidation,
- city war,
- political protection,
- hidden network period,
- crackdown and exile,
- reconstruction,
- regional expansion,
- or fragmentation.

Eras should emerge from sustained world state rather than being chosen from a fixed menu.

## Founders and successors

The founder should remain historically distinct even after many successions.

The chronicle should record:
- who founded the organization,
- original contacts and assets,
- founding district,
- initial purpose or ambition,
- and the first major turning point.

Successors inherit an existing institution, not a blank organization.

They may inherit:
- formal authority,
- assets,
- historical reputation,
- old obligations,
- institutional enemies,
- unresolved wars,
- and expectations created by former bosses.

They do not automatically inherit:
- personal trust,
- personal competence,
- intimate relationships,
- or the founder's exact reputation.

## Legendary members

Important NPCs may become legendary because of what actually happened during the campaign.

A character may become historically significant through:
- exceptional loyalty across many crises,
- decisive operational success,
- saving the organization from collapse,
- founding a major branch or business network,
- surviving extraordinary pressure,
- leading a successful succession,
- committing a defining betrayal,
- or becoming a long-term rival.

Legendary status should not require predefined hero classes.
It should emerge from repeated consequential actions and relationships.

The game should preserve both positive and negative legends.
A notorious traitor may be as important to the organization's history as its most loyal lieutenant.

## Historic operations

Routine operations should remain in operational records, but only selected operations should enter the permanent chronicle.

An operation may become historic when it:
- creates or destroys a major organization,
- wins or loses a strategic district,
- begins or ends a major war,
- exposes a boss or successor,
- creates a long-term income network,
- causes a major crackdown,
- saves the organization from collapse,
- completes an optional milestone,
- or produces an exceptional emergent chain of consequences.

The historical entry should emphasize consequences rather than tactical detail alone.

## Wars, rivalries, and diplomacy

The history system should preserve relationships over time.

For major rivals and allies, the chronicle may record:
- first contact,
- origin of hostility or cooperation,
- formal agreements,
- territorial disputes,
- defining operations,
- betrayals,
- periods of peace,
- subordination,
- absorption,
- destruction,
- or reconciliation.

This allows a rival to become a multi-generation historical actor rather than a temporary hostility value.

## Collapse and recovery

Near-total collapse should become part of the organization's identity.

The chronicle should record:
- what triggered the collapse,
- which assets and people were lost,
- who remained loyal,
- whether leadership fled, hid, or accepted subordination,
- how long the recovery lasted,
- and what restored the organization.

Recovery should be historically distinct from ordinary growth.
An organization rebuilt after destruction should feel older and more layered than a new organization of equal size.

## Historical reputation

History may influence how actors interpret the organization, but it should not become a universal permanent bonus system.

Relevant actors may remember:
- whether the organization keeps agreements,
- how it treats subordinates,
- whether it retaliates after betrayal,
- whether it survives crackdowns,
- whether successors maintain stability,
- and whether former allies tend to disappear or prosper.

Historical reputation should:
- depend on who knows the history,
- decay or become distorted over time,
- vary between districts and social groups,
- and be affected by propaganda, secrecy, and misinformation.

The same event may create fear among rivals, respect among recruits, and political hostility among institutions.

## Public, criminal, and secret history

Not every event should be equally visible.

### Public history

Known to civilians, media, or institutions.

Examples:
- public trials,
- visible violence,
- business empires,
- political scandals,
- major district unrest.

### Underworld history

Known mainly to criminal organizations and connected actors.

Examples:
- secret betrayals,
- informal truces,
- operational skill,
- internal succession disputes,
- hidden tribute arrangements.

### Secret history

Known only to a small number of characters or discovered through intelligence.

Examples:
- true responsibility for an operation,
- hidden informants,
- secret successors,
- covert institutional protection,
- fabricated rival attacks.

This distinction prevents the chronicle from revealing information the player or world actors should not know.

## Historical uncertainty

Some historical records may be incomplete, disputed, or false.

Possible states include:
- confirmed,
- widely believed,
- suspected,
- disputed,
- secret,
- and fabricated.

This supports:
- unreliable informants,
- propaganda,
- framed rivals,
- hidden betrayals,
- and later discoveries that reinterpret old events.

The organization timeline visible to the player may contain more information than any single world actor knows, but it should clearly distinguish certainty and secrecy.

## Chronicle presentation

The player should be able to review history through several connected views.

Possible views:
- chronological timeline,
- boss and successor lineage,
- reign summaries,
- legendary member biographies,
- map-based district history,
- organization relationship history,
- milestone gallery,
- collapse and recovery record,
- and final campaign report.

The primary presentation should remain readable and selective.
The game should avoid flooding the player with hundreds of low-value log entries.

## Milestones and titles

Optional sandbox milestones may create titles, badges, or historical labels.

Examples:
- Founder of the city's first dominant network,
- Survivor of a major crackdown,
- Architect of a sector monopoly,
- Unifier of rival districts,
- Builder of a multi-generation dynasty,
- or leader of a hidden organization that remained unidentified for decades.

These acknowledgements should primarily serve recognition and campaign identity.
They should not force the campaign to end and should not provide disproportionate permanent power.

## Organization identity

The organization should develop an identity from accumulated behavior.

Possible historical descriptors may emerge from:
- preferred operation types,
- treatment of allies and subordinates,
- use of violence,
- secrecy,
- economic specialization,
- succession stability,
- institutional relationships,
- and recovery patterns.

Examples:
- feared territorial organization,
- discreet financial network,
- unstable family dynasty,
- resilient exile syndicate,
- politically protected commercial empire,
- or betrayal-prone coalition.

These descriptors summarize history; they are not fixed character classes selected at campaign start.

## Legacy and succession

Legacy creates expectations for a successor.

A new boss may be compared with former leaders by:
- internal members,
- rivals,
- institutions,
- civilians,
- and the player.

A successor may choose to:
- continue the previous strategy,
- restore an older tradition,
- reject the founder's methods,
- repair historical relationships,
- or deliberately redefine the organization.

This creates a strategic and narrative tension between continuity and change.

## Legacy without deterministic bonuses

The history system should avoid simple rules such as:
- every former boss grants a permanent bonus,
- every historic operation increases all future success chances,
- or old organizations are automatically stronger than new ones.

History should matter through concrete world state:
- surviving contacts,
- inherited assets,
- known methods,
- reputation,
- unresolved obligations,
- institutional records,
- old enemies,
- and organizational traditions.

An old organization may be powerful, burdened, fragmented, respected, or nearly irrelevant depending on what survived.

## Campaign retirement report

When the player voluntarily retires a campaign or reaches true game over, the game should generate a historical report.

The report may include:
- organization name and founding date,
- founder,
- full leadership sequence,
- duration of each reign,
- greatest territorial reach,
- major sectors and businesses,
- largest income period,
- most important allies and rivals,
- defining wars and operations,
- major betrayals,
- investigations and crackdowns survived,
- collapses and recoveries,
- legendary members,
- completed optional milestones,
- final organization condition,
- and reason the chronicle ended.

A voluntarily retired campaign should be presented as a completed history, not a defeat.

## MVP boundary

The MVP does not require the full legacy system.

For the MVP, it is enough to record:
- organization founding,
- current boss,
- important operations,
- major district gains and losses,
- significant arrests or deaths,
- optional local milestones,
- and the final game-over event.

The complete reign, era, uncertainty, biography, and multi-generation systems belong to later development stages.

## Design principle

Organization history is well designed if the player can look back at the campaign and understand how the current world emerged from earlier decisions.

The chronicle should not merely repeat statistics.
It should preserve causality, people, turning points, and consequences.

The organization becomes meaningful when it has a past that continues to shape its present.

## Open Questions

- What exact impact threshold should promote an event into the permanent chronicle?
- Should the player be able to manually mark events or characters as historically important?
- How should conflicting accounts of the same event be presented?
- Can propaganda deliberately rewrite public history while secret records retain the truth?
- How much historical information should a new successor immediately know?
- Should organization names, symbols, or doctrines be allowed to change while preserving continuity?
- Which history views are required for the first post-MVP version?

## Future Extensions

- Shareable campaign chronicles.
- Automatically generated biographies of bosses and legendary members.
- Exportable timeline and dynasty reports.
- Historical comparison between different campaigns.
- Memorials, named businesses, streets, or internal organization traditions.
- Rumors and myths that transform real events over generations.
- Cultural memory differences between districts, organizations, and institutions.
- Player-authored notes attached to historical entries.
