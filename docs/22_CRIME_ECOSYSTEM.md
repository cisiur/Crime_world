# Crime Ecosystem

## Purpose

This document describes how the illegal side of the world works as a connected system.

The criminal ecosystem is not just a list of crimes.
It is a web of sources, routes, intermediaries, risks, and payoffs.

## Core idea

Illegal activity should connect to legal activity.
The criminal ecosystem should grow inside the gaps and weaknesses of the legal one.

## Main criminal functions

The criminal ecosystem needs to answer:
- where illegal goods come from,
- how they move,
- where they are stored,
- who protects them,
- who buys them,
- how money is cleaned,
- how risk is hidden,
- how rival groups compete for the same flows.

## Main crime domains

The game can support multiple criminal domains, but the MVP should keep them small and readable.

Examples:
- theft
- extortion
- robbery
- smuggling
- drug production
- drug distribution
- bribery
- blackmail
- protection rackets
- sabotage
- infiltration
- asset takeover

## Supply chain logic

Crime should have supply chains just like the legal economy.

Example chain:
source -> transport -> storage -> distribution -> profit -> laundering -> expansion

Another chain:
weak district -> recruitment -> street crime -> pressure -> corruption -> opportunity

Another chain:
legal business -> hidden control -> laundering -> criminal funding -> influence

## Criminal resources

Criminal organizations should rely on resources such as:
- trusted people
- vehicles
- safe storage
- weapons or force capability
- money
- information
- corruption links
- front businesses
- territorial access

## Criminal intermediaries

Not every crime should happen directly.
The ecosystem can include intermediaries such as:
- couriers
- fixers
- informants
- corrupt officials
- business fronts
- transport operators
- security teams
- accountants

## Risk distribution

Illegal systems should spread risk across the network.

A strong ecosystem is one where a loss in one place does not destroy the whole chain.
A weak ecosystem is one where one raid or betrayal can collapse everything.

## Connection to legal economy

The criminal ecosystem should plug into legal systems through:
- transport
- retail
- hospitality
- real estate
- industry
- finance
- media
- construction

Legal assets can hide, enable, or stabilize illegal activity.

## Pressure interaction

The more visible a criminal chain is, the more pressure it creates.

Pressure can come from:
- police
- rivals
- public fear
- political pressure
- internal distrust
- economic disruption

## Competition inside the ecosystem

Rivals should compete over:
- routes
- districts
- suppliers
- labor
- storage
- market access
- laundering paths
- corruption contacts

## Player role in the ecosystem

The player is not the entire ecosystem.
The player is an actor trying to carve out control inside it.

The player can:
- create new routes,
- steal existing routes,
- hide operations inside legal assets,
- break rival chains,
- stabilize their own chain,
- expand from local flow to regional flow.

## Design principle

Crime should feel systemic, not cosmetic.
If illegal activity does not create ripple effects in the economy, districts, pressure, and rival behavior, it is too shallow.
