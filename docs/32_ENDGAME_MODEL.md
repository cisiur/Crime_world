# Endgame Model

## Purpose

This document defines how CrimeWorld handles victory, failure, collapse, recovery, and endless play.

CrimeWorld is an open-ended sandbox.
The game should recognize major achievements without forcing the campaign to end.

## Core principle

There is no mandatory victory screen and no fixed campaign deadline.

The player may pursue any ambition, ignore suggested goals, complete several different ambitions, or continue playing indefinitely.

The only true campaign-ending failure is the death of the current boss when no valid successor can take control.

## Campaign continuation

A campaign continues through:
- territorial loss,
- bankruptcy,
- arrests,
- prison sentences,
- business collapse,
- loss of influence,
- organizational fragmentation,
- rival domination,
- and temporary removal of the boss from active leadership.

These are severe setbacks, but they are not automatic game-over states.

The organization may shrink back to a small crew or underground network and later rebuild.

## True game-over condition

The campaign ends when all of the following are true:

1. The current boss dies.
2. No valid successor is available.
3. No organizational mechanism can lawfully or practically transfer control to a new playable leader.

A valid successor may be:
- a designated heir,
- a family member,
- a trusted lieutenant,
- an elected internal leader,
- or another character recognized by the organization's succession rules.

If a successor exists, the death of the boss ends a reign, not the campaign.

## Boss death and succession

When the boss dies and a successor exists:
- control transfers to the successor,
- personal assets and contacts are inherited according to existing relationships and legal structures,
- some reputation transfers to the successor,
- personal trust does not transfer automatically,
- internal loyalty is recalculated,
- rivals may test the new leadership,
- institutions may reopen old opportunities or investigations,
- and the organization may enter a succession crisis.

Succession should preserve continuity while creating meaningful instability.

## Imprisonment

Imprisonment does not end the campaign.

Depending on sentence length, influence, and organizational strength:
- the boss may continue issuing limited orders,
- a lieutenant may act as temporary leader,
- the organization may operate autonomously,
- loyalty may decay,
- rivals may attempt to exploit the absence,
- and succession pressure may increase.

A permanent sentence can effectively remove the boss from operational play without ending the campaign, provided leadership can continue and the boss is still alive.

## Collapse without game over

The player can suffer near-total collapse and continue.

Possible collapse states include:
- loss of all controlled districts,
- destruction or seizure of legal businesses,
- disappearance of recurring income,
- loss of most members,
- broken alliances,
- extreme exposure,
- and forced relocation.

Recovery should remain possible if the player still controls a living boss or valid successor and retains any plausible path back into the simulation.

Possible recovery tools include:
- going underground,
- relying on personal contacts,
- rebuilding from a safe location,
- operating through small low-visibility actions,
- accepting temporary subordination,
- relocating to another district or city,
- negotiating protection,
- and recruiting from former networks.

## Recovery philosophy

Recovery should be difficult but strategically interesting.

The game should avoid two extremes:
- irreversible snowball failure after one major setback,
- and consequence-free recovery that makes collapse meaningless.

A player rebuilding after collapse should face:
- reduced capability,
- damaged trust,
- weakened reputation,
- stronger rivals,
- lost infrastructure,
- and fewer safe options.

However, the player may retain:
- knowledge,
- personal relationships,
- historical reputation,
- hidden assets,
- and organizational memory.

These retained advantages make recovery different from starting a new campaign.

## Optional sandbox goals

CrimeWorld may present optional ambitions and major milestones.

Examples include:
- dominate a city,
- become the most influential organization in a region,
- monopolize a criminal or legal sector,
- control key transport routes,
- build the richest organization,
- become politically protected,
- survive a major coordinated crackdown,
- defeat or subordinate every major rival in a city,
- create a durable multi-generation dynasty,
- establish a multi-city network,
- or remain a hidden organization with minimal exposure for decades.

These goals are not required.
The player may pursue several, abandon them, or define their own objectives.

## Milestone completion

Completing an optional goal should:
- be clearly recognized,
- create a historical record,
- provide a campaign summary for that achievement,
- unlock a badge, title, statistic, or narrative acknowledgement where appropriate,
- and leave the campaign running without changing its fundamental rules.

Completing one goal does not prevent the player from pursuing others.

The game should never force the player to stop after reaching a milestone.

## Endless play

After any achievement, the simulation continues normally.

The world should continue to:
- generate new organizations,
- create new leaders,
- change district economies,
- open and close businesses,
- produce political and institutional shifts,
- generate new rivalries,
- react to succession,
- and challenge dominant organizations.

Long-term dominance should change the nature of difficulty rather than remove it.

A dominant player should face:
- larger coalitions,
- deeper investigations,
- internal fragmentation,
- succession instability,
- dependency on complex systems,
- and the difficulty of maintaining control across distance and time.

## No final invulnerability

No achievement should make the organization permanently untouchable.

Even the strongest organization remains vulnerable to:
- betrayal,
- poor succession,
- institutional change,
- economic disruption,
- coordinated rivals,
- exposed dependencies,
- leadership mistakes,
- and accumulated internal instability.

Endless play requires ongoing tension.

## Player-defined endings

The player may voluntarily retire a campaign at any time.

A voluntary ending should generate a final historical summary including:
- length of the campaign,
- bosses and successors,
- greatest territorial reach,
- major businesses and income sources,
- biggest rivals,
- alliances and betrayals,
- major investigations and crackdowns,
- recoveries from collapse,
- completed milestones,
- and the final condition of the organization.

Voluntary retirement does not count as defeat.
It is the player choosing where the story ends.

## Organization history and legacy

The campaign should maintain a persistent historical record.

Important historical entries may include:
- organization founding,
- each boss's reign,
- succession events,
- major wars,
- decisive operations,
- legendary members,
- major betrayals,
- lost and regained territories,
- historic businesses,
- major raids and investigations,
- milestone completions,
- and periods of collapse and recovery.

This history makes endless play meaningful by turning the organization into a long-term institution rather than a temporary collection of statistics.

The detailed history and legacy system should be defined in a dedicated follow-up document.

## Endgame and progression

The progression model describes increasing capability.
The endgame model describes what the player does after reaching major capability.

Late-game play should focus less on unlocking basic actions and more on:
- maintaining complex networks,
- managing successors,
- protecting dependencies,
- shaping institutions,
- controlling subordinate organizations,
- responding to coalitions,
- and surviving the consequences of long-term power.

## Endgame and pressure

Pressure remains active at every stage.

Large organizations should generate different pressure than small ones:
- broader investigations,
- political campaigns,
- financial scrutiny,
- coordinated operations,
- international attention,
- and rival coalitions.

The late game should not simply multiply district patrol numbers.
It should create new categories of strategic threat.

## Endgame and diplomacy

Diplomacy becomes increasingly important as direct control expands.

A dominant organization may need to:
- maintain subordinate groups,
- prevent hostile coalitions,
- manage tribute relationships,
- protect institutional intermediaries,
- and balance rivals against one another.

A player who relies only on direct violence should find large-scale control unstable and expensive.

## MVP boundary

The MVP does not need the full endgame system.

For the MVP, it is enough to validate:
- optional local milestones,
- campaign continuation after major setbacks,
- no automatic loss from bankruptcy or territorial collapse,
- and a clear game-over condition tied to boss death without a successor.

Full dynasty history, multi-city recovery, and advanced late-game threats belong to later development stages.

## Design principle

The endgame is well designed if the player can achieve something extraordinary without the game telling them to stop.

Failure should end the campaign only when the living chain of leadership is broken.

Everything else should become history, consequence, and an opportunity to rebuild.

## Open Questions

- What minimum requirements make a character a valid successor?
- Can an organization appoint an emergency successor after the boss has already become incapacitated?
- How long can an imprisoned boss remain the formal leader before a succession crisis becomes unavoidable?
- Which optional milestones should exist in the first playable version?
- Should voluntary retirement create a shareable campaign report?
- How should multi-city relocation work after complete local collapse?

## Future Extensions

- A dedicated organization history and legacy model.
- Detailed dynasty and family succession systems.
- Historical eras and generational change.
- Multi-city exile and return campaigns.
- Player-created campaign objectives.
- Post-campaign comparison statistics and organization chronicles.
